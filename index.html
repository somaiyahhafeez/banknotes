Cash Gap: More Men Than Women on Banknotes.

Only 21% of banknotes feature a woman.

By Somaiyah Hafeez

Times have changed since paper currency in China during the 7th century, less and less people carry cash and almost everything banking has shifted to the digital world. Yet the underrepresentation of women in every aspect of life remains somewhat constant. Women on banknotes, in the 21st century, remain a rare sight. Data collected from 38 countries by The Pudding shows that only 21% of banknotes feature a woman.

Even after her death, Queen Elizabeth II remains the most featured face on currencies around the world. More than 35 nations have featured her on their money since 1935. Canada first issued a banknote featuring Elizabeth when she was just 8 years old. Even after her death in 2022, eight Commonwealth nations continue to feature her on their banknotes along with one former Commonwealth member and ten dependent territories.

    Australia features the most women on their banknotes other than Queen Elizabeth II:

  • Mary Reibey: Pioneer businesswoman with interests in shipping and property.
  • Catherine Helen Spence: Journalist, social and political reformer, novelist and feminist.
  • Dame Nellie Melba :World-renowned soprano.
  • Dame Mary Gilmore:https://banknotes.rba.gov.au/australias-banknotes/people-on-the-banknotes/dame-mary-gilmore/

However after the Queen's death Australia announced that it would remove the British monarchy from its banknotes. Until a new design is finalized, the $5 Australian bill will remain to feature her.

It took Caroline Criado-Perez, a British author and journalist, an almost legal battle with the Bank of England and online abuse to get Jane Austen on the £10 bill in England. When the Bank of England announced in 2013 that Winston Churchill would replace Elizabeth Fry, a British prison and social reformer, on the £5 note from 2016, Perez knew it was a “matter of principle” for her to fight to get more women on banknotes.
